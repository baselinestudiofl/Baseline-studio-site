How to Start an Event Production Business:
Lessons from 10+ Years in Miami

By Saul Leon, Founder Published March 20, 2026 Miami, FL

Baseline Studio was founded in 2017 with a clear idea of what South Florida events were missing. Not another budget DJ service — a real production company that treats every event like a production. Today that means a full team, in-house lighting and FX inventory, professional-grade audio, and a client portal that manages the whole workflow.

The distance between those two points wasn't a straight line. Here's what we'd tell our younger selves — and what anyone thinking about starting an event production business should understand going in.

Define What You're Actually Building

Event production is a broad term. It can mean a solo DJ with a couple of speakers. It can mean a 15-person crew with a warehouse full of lighting inventory. Before you spend a dollar on gear, get clear on what you're building and who you're building it for.

The businesses that fail early are almost always the ones that tried to serve everyone. They said yes to the backyard house party and the luxury wedding in the same weekend and couldn't build a reputation in either market. Pick a lane. That clarity shapes every decision — what gear you invest in, how you price, where you show up, what you turn down.

Gear Strategy: Buy vs. Rent Until You Know

The temptation when starting is to buy everything. Don't. In the early years, rent what you don't have. The rental cost eats your margin, but it gives you the flexibility to take on varied events while you figure out what gear you actually need at high frequency.

When you find yourself renting the same piece of equipment for five consecutive events, that's your signal to buy it. That's data — not guesswork.

We also rented before buying in specific categories on purpose — testing speaker brands, lighting fixtures, and console setups before committing. Your taste and technical standards will evolve faster than you expect in the first two years.

Staffing: The Hardest Part of Scaling

The transition from "me doing everything" to "a team doing this" is where most event businesses plateau or fail. You have to hire before you feel ready — because if you wait until you're overwhelmed, you'll make bad hires under pressure.

What we look for in production staff:

  • Technical competence — can they actually do the job?
  • Reliability above everything else. The events industry has zero tolerance for no-shows.
  • Communication — can they represent the company professionally in front of clients and venue partners?
  • Calm under pressure. Things go sideways at live events. Who do you want handling it?

Train everyone to your standards before they're in front of a client. There are no dress rehearsals in live events.

Build Your Reputation Intentionally

In this industry, your reputation is your marketing budget. Every event coordinator in Miami talks to every other coordinator. If you're the vendor who shows up late or causes friction with the venue team, that reputation spreads faster than any bad review. If you're the vendor who is calm, overprepared, and makes everyone else's job easier, that also spreads — and turns into referrals.

Specific things that build reputation faster than anything else:

  • Advance venue visits. Showing up before event day signals you take your craft seriously.
  • Detailed run-of-show documents. Sending the coordinator a production timeline they can actually work from makes their job easier and makes you look professional.
  • Clean load-ins and load-outs. How you treat a venue's space says everything about your company.
  • Follow up after every event. A check-in message a week later costs nothing and generates reviews and referrals consistently.

Pricing for Sustainability

Most new production businesses make the same mistake: they underprice to compete. Know your real cost of doing business — gear depreciation and maintenance, insurance, vehicle costs, overhead, and your own time for planning, communication, and execution. When you know your floor, you can price above it with confidence.

Clients who understand value will pay for it. Clients who only want the lowest price are usually the most demanding and difficult to work with anyway. See how we structure our pricing on our services page.

Technology Is Your Leverage

Invest in the software infrastructure early: a proper CRM and contract management system, online payment processing, and a client portal where questionnaires, timelines, and communications live in one place. This isn't overhead — it's the difference between looking like a professional company and looking like a side hustle. Clients notice, and it reduces the communication burden on you dramatically as you scale.

The Long Game

Building an event production business from scratch takes longer than most people expect. The first year is about survival. The second is about refining. The third year is when compounding starts — referrals from couples whose friends are now getting married, venue relationships generating consistent bookings, a reputation that does some of the selling for you.

Stay patient. Stay disciplined. Say no to work that doesn't fit your direction, even when you need the revenue. Every event you produce is either building or eroding the reputation you're working to establish. Learn more about how we built ours on our about page.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to start an event production business?

Starting a full event production company in South Florida requires substantial equipment investment to be competitive — DJ gear, a PA system, uplighting fixtures, moving heads, and a cargo vehicle. Most successful operators start with one specialization and expand as revenue grows. Don't try to buy everything at once.

What licenses do I need to run an event production company in Miami?

You need a Florida business license, a Miami-Dade business tax receipt if operating locally, general liability insurance (most venues require proof before load-in), and for pyrotechnic effects like cold sparks and CO2, a licensed operator on your team or properly subcontracted.

How do event production companies find clients in Miami?

The most effective channels are Google SEO for local searches, referrals from wedding planners and venue coordinators, and Instagram/video marketing showing real event footage. Most established companies get 60–70% of new business from referrals after 3–5 years of consistent quality work.
